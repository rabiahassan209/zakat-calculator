❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Clear answers to the questions most people ask about Zakat

Zakat is one of the Five Pillars of Islam. It is a mandatory form of charity that requires Muslims to give a set portion of their wealth to those in need each year.

The word Zakat means purification and growth. By paying Zakat, Muslims purify their wealth, fulfil a religious obligation, and help support the poor and vulnerable in their community.

In general, Zakat is obligatory for Muslims who own wealth above the Nisab threshold for one full lunar year (known as the Hawl).

The basic conditions are: you are an adult Muslim, you are of sound mind, and your total eligible wealth has remained above the Nisab for one lunar year. Scholarly opinions on specific circumstances may vary, so consult a scholar if you are unsure.

Nisab is the minimum amount of wealth a Muslim must own before Zakat becomes obligatory. It is set at the equivalent value of either:

  • 85 grams of gold, or
  • 595 grams of silver

Many contemporary scholars and Islamic organisations use the silver Nisab because it results in a lower threshold, meaning more people become eligible to pay Zakat. This calculator uses the silver Nisab by default.

The following types of wealth are generally considered eligible for Zakat:

  • Cash in hand and bank savings
  • Gold and silver, including eligible jewelry
  • Business cash and inventory (goods held for sale)
  • Money owed to you that you reasonably expect to receive
  • Investments and shares
  • Property purchased for resale (not your home)
  • Rental income you still have on your Zakat date

The following are generally excluded from Zakat calculations:

  • Your primary home where you live
  • Your personal vehicle used for daily travel
  • Everyday clothing and personal belongings
  • Household furniture and appliances
  • Property or equipment used in your business (not held for resale)

Zakat

What is Zakat? The Third Pillar of Islam

5 min read

Zakat is one of the Five Pillars of Islam, alongside the declaration of faith, prayer, fasting, and Hajj. It is a mandatory act of worship in which Muslims give a fixed portion of their wealth — 2.5% — to those in need each year.

Allah says in the Quran: "Take from their wealth a charity by which you purify and cause them increase, and invoke [Allah's blessings] upon them." (Surah At-Tawbah, 9:103)

Why is Zakat Important?

Zakat means both purification and growth. It purifies the wealth of the giver from greed and excess, and it purifies the heart from attachment to material things. At the same time, it helps wealth circulate through society, supporting the poor, the needy, and other eligible categories mentioned in the Quran.

Who Must Pay Zakat?

Zakat becomes obligatory on a Muslim who is an adult, of sound mind, and owns wealth above the Nisab threshold for one full lunar year. This includes cash, gold, silver, business assets, and certain investments.

A Hadith on Zakat

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Islam is built upon five [pillars]: testifying that there is no god but Allah and that Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah, establishing the prayer, paying the Zakat, making the pilgrimage to the House, and fasting in Ramadan." (Sahih al-Bukhari 8, Sahih Muslim 16)

Fitrana

Understanding Fitrana (Zakat al-Fitr)

4 min read

Fitrana, also called Zakat al-Fitr or Sadaqah al-Fitr, is a charity that every Muslim must give at the end of Ramadan, before the Eid al-Fitr prayer. Unlike annual Zakat, Fitrana is not based on wealth — it is a fixed amount paid on behalf of every person in the household.

Why is Fitrana Given?

Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) reported that the Prophet ﷺ made Zakat al-Fitr obligatory as a purification for the fasting person from idle talk and obscenity, and as food for the needy. (Sunan Abu Dawud 1609)

Who Must Pay It?

Every Muslim must pay Fitrana on behalf of themselves and their dependents — including children, the elderly, and even guests staying overnight on the eve of Eid — as long as they have food beyond their basic needs for that day.

How Much is Fitrana?

The traditional amount is one Sa' (roughly 2.5 to 3 kg) of a staple food such as wheat, barley, dates, or raisins, per person. Many scholars allow paying the cash equivalent based on current local food prices.

When Must It Be Paid?

Fitrana can be paid any time during Ramadan, but it must reach the needy before the Eid al-Fitr prayer begins. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever pays it before the prayer, it is an accepted Zakat, and whoever pays it after the prayer, it is [ordinary] charity." (Sunan Abu Dawud 1609)

Nisab

What is Nisab and Why Does It Matter?

4 min read

Nisab is the minimum amount of wealth a Muslim must own before Zakat becomes obligatory. If your wealth is below the Nisab, you are not required to pay Zakat that year. If it is above the Nisab and has remained so for one full lunar year, Zakat of 2.5% becomes due.

The Two Nisab Thresholds

Nisab is traditionally measured using two standards:

  • Gold Nisab — equivalent to 85 grams of gold
  • Silver Nisab — equivalent to 595 grams of silver

Why Use the Silver Nisab?

Because silver is generally less valuable than gold, the silver Nisab results in a lower wealth threshold. This means more people become eligible to pay Zakat, which directs more support toward the poor and needy. Many contemporary scholars and Islamic organisations recommend using the silver Nisab for this reason.

A Foundational Hadith

The Prophet ﷺ said: "There is no Zakat on wealth less than five Awqiyyah [of silver]." (Sahih al-Bukhari 1447, Sahih Muslim 979) This hadith forms the basis for the silver Nisab calculation used by scholars today.

Hadith

What the Prophet ﷺ Said About Charity

5 min read

Charity holds a central place in Islamic teachings. The Quran and the sayings of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ repeatedly emphasise the spiritual and social rewards of giving. Here are some authentic ahadith that highlight the virtue of charity.

Charity Does Not Decrease Wealth

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Charity does not decrease wealth, no one forgives another except that Allah increases his honour, and no one humbles himself for the sake of Allah except that Allah raises his status." (Sahih Muslim 2588)

Even a Smile is Charity

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Your smile for your brother is charity." (Sunan al-Tirmidhi 1956) This hadith reminds us that charity is not limited to money — kindness in all its forms carries reward.

The Shade of Charity

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Every person will be in the shade of their charity on the Day of Judgement, until the people have been judged." (Musnad Ahmad 17170, graded sound by scholars) This highlights the lasting reward of charity in the Hereafter.

Give Even a Little

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Save yourself from Hellfire even by giving half a date in charity." (Sahih al-Bukhari 1417, Sahih Muslim 1016) No act of charity is too small to matter.

Putting It Into Practice

These teachings show that charity — whether obligatory like Zakat and Fitrana, or voluntary like Sadaqah — is deeply woven into the Islamic way of life. Use our calculators to fulfill your obligations accurately and with confidence.

Sadaqah Jariyah

Sadaqah Jariyah: Charity That Never Stops

5 min read

Most good deeds end when a person passes away. But Islam describes one special category of charity whose reward keeps flowing even after death — known as Sadaqah Jariyah, or ongoing charity.

The Core Hadith

Abu Hurairah (may Allah be pleased with him) reported that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said: "When a man dies, his deeds come to an end except for three: ongoing charity, beneficial knowledge, or a righteous child who prays for him." (Sahih Muslim 1631)

This hadith explains why Sadaqah Jariyah is so highly valued — it is one of the very few ways a believer's good deeds can continue to grow in reward, by Allah's permission, long after they are gone.

What Counts as Sadaqah Jariyah?

Scholars commonly point to examples such as:

  • Building or supporting a mosque
  • Digging a well or providing a clean water source
  • Planting a tree that people or animals benefit from
  • Funding education, books, or a copy of the Quran for others to use
  • Building a shelter for travelers or those in need

A Hadith on Planting

The Prophet ﷺ said: "If a Muslim plants a tree or sows a field, and a human, bird, or animal eats from it, it is regarded as a charitable gift for him." (Sahih al-Bukhari 2320, Sahih Muslim 1553)

A Hadith on Wells

It is reported that the Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever digs a well will receive reward as long as any living creature drinks from it." Many scholars relate this to the well dug by Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him), one of the most famous examples of Sadaqah Jariyah in Islamic history.

Why It Matters

Sadaqah Jariyah encourages believers to think beyond a single act of charity, and instead invest in something lasting — whether for themselves or on behalf of a deceased loved one. Many Muslims give Sadaqah Jariyah on behalf of their parents so that the reward continues to reach them after death.

While Sadaqah Jariyah is usually given through a lasting project, it is part of the same spirit of generosity behind Zakat and Fitrana — using your wealth to bring lasting benefit to others.

5 Steps to Prepare Your Zakat Before Ramadan

4 min read

Many Muslims choose to calculate and pay their Zakat during Ramadan, as good deeds are multiplied in reward during this blessed month. Preparing ahead of time makes the process easier and more accurate. Here are five simple steps to get ready.

1
List all your assets — cash, bank savings, gold, silver, business stock, and investments.
2
Check today's gold and silver prices so your Nisab threshold is accurate.
3
Note down any outstanding debts or bills that are due — these can be deducted.
4
Use a reliable Zakat Calculator to work out your exact 2.5% obligation.
5
Choose a trustworthy place to give your Zakat — local mosques, registered charities, or directly to those in need.

Why Many Pay Zakat in Ramadan

While Zakat can be paid at any point once a full lunar year has passed on your wealth, many Muslims prefer to calculate and distribute it during Ramadan because of the multiplied rewards available in this month, and because it helps the needy have what they require for Ramadan and Eid.

A Reminder on Charity

The Prophet ﷺ said: "The most beloved of deeds to Allah are those done most consistently, even if they are small." (Sahih al-Bukhari 6464, Sahih Muslim 783) Preparing your Zakat calmly and accurately, rather than rushing at the last moment, reflects this spirit of consistency and care.
